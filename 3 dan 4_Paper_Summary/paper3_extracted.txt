Spatial Autoregressive Models for Geographically
Hierarchical Data Structures
Guanpeng Dong, Richard Harris
School of Geographical Sciences, University of Bristol, Bristol, U.K.
This article discusses how standard spatial autoregressive models and their estimation can
be extended to accommodate geographically hierarchical data structures. Whereas stan-
dard spatial econometric models normally operate at a single geographical scale, many
geographical data sets are hierarchical in nature—for example, information about houses
nested into data about the census tracts in which those houses are found. Here we outline
four model speciﬁcations by combining different formulations of the spatial weight matrix
W and of ways of modeling regional effects. These are (1) groupwise W and ﬁxed regional
effects; (2) groupwise W and random regional effects; (3) proximity-based W and ﬁxed
regional effects; and (4) proximity-based W and random regional effects. We discuss each
of these model speciﬁcations and their associated estimation methods, giving particular
attention to the fourth. We describe this as a hierarchical spatial autoregressive model. We
view it as having the most potential to extend spatial econometrics to accommodate
geographically hierarchical data structures and as offering the greatest coming together of
spatial econometric and multilevel modeling approaches. Subsequently, we provide Bayes-
ian Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithms for implementing the model. We demonstrate its
application using a two-level land price data set where land parcels nest into districts in
Beijing, China, ﬁnding signiﬁcant spatial dependence at both the land parcel level and the
district level.
Introduction
Many geographical data sets have hierarchical structures—for example, houses nested into
districts into regions in an urban housing market (Jones 1991). Using the language of the
multilevel modeling literature (Goldstein 2003), the ﬁner spatial scale at which outcomes of
interest are measured is termed the lower level, and the higher levels are the more aggregate
spatial scales such as regions. The assumption of multilevel modeling is that there is both
difference between objects at the higher level and some level of group dependence within those
objects. In other words, difference between regions and correlations within regions are expected:

the outcomes of lower level units in the same region tend to be correlated because they are
impacted by the same effects. This can be described as vertical group dependence.
However, horizontal dependence cannot be modeled using classic multilevel approaches.
This is the sort of spatial dependence associated with spatial econometric modeling of single-
level spatial data sets; it arises from the interactions or spillovers among spatial entities due to
their geographic proximity. When working with geographically hierarchical data sets, we can
expect (or at least allow for) both types of dependence: the vertical and the horizontal. The ﬁrst,
a top-down group dependence, is due to regional (or contextual) effects; the second to the
traditional spatial dependence concern of spatial econometrics. Essentially, the distinction we are
making is between spatial interaction (at lower levels) and spatial heterogeneity (at regional
levels). Deﬁned as such, both types of dependency are clearly spatial. However, the phrase group
dependency acknowledges that the allocation of individuals to regions need not be geographical
(hierarchical models need not be modeling a geographical hierarchy).
Conceptually, in this article, we distinguish these two types of dependency by providing a
general methodological framework for simultaneously modeling the spatial interaction and
regional effects. The merits associated with this methodology are several. Generally, it will
provide more efﬁcient and accurate model estimation for regression coefﬁcients by recognizing
the hierarchical data structure. The regional effects estimated by the model can be mapped to
explicitly investigate the spatial patterns in the outcomes of interest at the regional level. In
addition, it provides more accurate estimates of the intensity of spatial interaction at the
lower level by separating measurement from the regional effects with which it may be
confounded.
We build the methodological framework based on the standard spatial econometric
methods, especially the spatial simultaneous autoregressive model (Cliff and Ord 1981; Anselin
1988; Grifﬁth 1988; Haining 2003; LeSage and Pace 2009). Within spatial econometrics, there
has been interest in the use of hierarchical data sets where regional variables are used to control
possible regional effects when modeling outcomes observed at a subregional scale. For
example, when modeling house prices, regional-level variables such as crime rates, school
quality, or socioeconomic description are incorporated into the model using data for census
tracts or districts (see, e.g., Bowen, Mikelbank, and Prestegaard 2001; Baumont 2009). The
logic is that the spatial interaction effect indicated by a signiﬁcant autoregressive parameter (in
a lagged y model: LeSage and Pace 2009, pp. 16–20) might merely be a statistical artifact
created by ignoring regional effects. Unfortunately, simply adding regional variables is not
sufﬁcient to model the outcome of interest appropriately. Treating a regional covariate as a
repeated measurement, whereby all individuals within the same region are assigned identical
values of the regional variable, will lead to inefﬁcient model estimation and incorrect statistical
inference (Goldstein 2003).
Given the prevalence of geographically hierarchical data sets, it is surprising that studies on
modifying standard spatial autoregressive (SAR) models to better accommodate a geographically
hierarchical structure remain rare (exceptions being Smith and LeSage 2004; Corrado and
Fingleton 2012; LeSage and Llano 2013). In the context of modeling 1996 presidential election
results for U.S. counties, Smith and LeSage (2004) build a spatial probit model in which election
outcomes of each county are not independent of each other. The dependence is due to these
counties being taken to be nested into U.S. states. It is assumed that counties within the same state
share the same state-level (regional) effect. Moreover, these regional effects themselves are
spatially dependent at the state level. There are spatial patterns to voting at the state level, and the

interaction between these state-level voting behaviors is estimated with a spatial interaction term.
What is not considered is the equivalent interaction at a county level. The assumption that the
lower level interaction can be discarded or is subsumed by the higher level interaction is not
reasonable in many situations (Case 1992; McMillen 1992; LeSage 2000; LeSage et al. 2011).
Corrado and Fingleton (2012) propose an approach to estimate spatial dependence among lower
level units (speciﬁed in a groupwise way, see below) by ﬁnding valid instrumental variables for
the endogenous lagged outcome variable. The concern here is that their groupwise speciﬁcation
of lower level spatial dependence violates the notion of distance decay, the core concept in spatial
analysis (Getis and Ord 1992; Haining, Kerry, and Oliver 2010; Anselin and Arribas-Bel 2013).
Moreover, the regional effects in their framework are assumed to be independent rather than
dependent.
What we are proposing is similar to the spatial misalignment perspective on hierarchical
structures of spatial data that is found in the spatial statistics literature. The problem of spatial
misalignment, or a change of support problem, refers to analysis or inference on a variable at one
spatial scale based on data at another spatial hierarchy, or on relationships between spatial
variables at different scales (Gotway and Young 2002; Banerjee, Carlin, and Gelfand 2004, p.
175). For example, in an analysis of the impact of air pollution on health outcomes, the air
pollution data are from a number of monitoring locations scattered across the study area whereas
the health outcomes are only available at the scale of census tracts. The solution is in two steps
(Agarwal, Gelfand, and Silander 2002; Zhu, Carlin, and Gelfand 2003). The ﬁrst step is to
“realign” variables to the same spatial scale by interpolating census tract-level air pollution from
point-level information (using methodologies described by Mugglin and Carlin 1998; Gelfand,
Zhu, and Carlin 2001). The next step is to relate the health outcomes to the newly realigned
census tract air pollution variable.
In particular, Banerjee, Carlin, and Gelfand (2004, pp. 206–212) demonstrate a two-level
spatially misaligned regression model where the interest is to explain the relationship between
land uses and population at the pixel level but with a key problem being that the population
variable is only observed at the town level. They have constructed a full Bayesian framework for
estimating such types of spatial multilevel models to capture the impact of the latent pixel-level
population on land uses by conditioning on the higher level town population. The similarities
between their framework and our approach are in modeling a spatially hierarchical data structure
and examining the impacts of covariates at both the lower and higher levels on outcomes
measured at the lower level. However, several differences can also be identiﬁed. First, their higher
level variable plays an important and explicit role in inferring the relationship between the
corresponding unobserved variable at the lower level and the outcome variable. By contrast, in
our approach, the higher level covariate effects are directly interpreted as the observed contextual
effects on outcomes measured at the lower level in a way that is similar with a typical regression
model. Another important difference is that spatial interaction effects at the lower level are not
modeled in their framework, which is a major concern in this article.
Proximity-based and groupwise spatial weight matrices and hierarchical
data structures
In spatial econometrics, the spatial connectivity matrix, W, speciﬁes the interaction structure
among the modeled entities. It deﬁnes the connection between those entities that will be exam-
ined for spatial dependence. Most often the strength of spatial dependency is assumed to be
proximity based, deﬁned by geographic distance, nearest neighbors, or by contiguity (see detailed

deﬁne purely groupwise effects, regarding entities within the same region as neighbors of each
other and leaving entities between regions as unconnected. This groupwise W has been discussed
extensively in the social interaction literature where the primary interest is to estimate the
endogenous group effects or peer effects on an individual’s behavior (Manski 1993; Durlauf and
Ioannides 2010; Lin 2010). The statistical properties of estimators using this groupwise W have
been investigated by Lee (2007; see also Kelejian and Prucha 2002; Lin 2010; Anselin and
Arribas-Bel 2013). The essential departure of the groupwise spatial dependence from the
proximity-based dependence is that the former conceptualizes spatial dependence as discontinu-
ous, bounded by regional boundaries. Consequently, two lower level units located either side of
a boundary are assumed independent even though they are in close proximity.

Estimating the HSAR model
As indicated by the references in the preceding discussion, the estimation of model (a), (b) and
(c) has been investigated. However, to the best of our knowledge, the HSAR model has not been
discussed before. Therefore, in this section, we provide Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo
(MCMC) algorithms for implementing it. The application of Bayesian estimation methods in
spatial econometrics has been discussed by many researchers (inter alia, Hepple 1995a; LeSage
1997; LeSage and Pace 2009). The Bayesian MCMC method is preferred over MLE in the spatial
econometrics literatures for implementing complex models such as those with a complex
variance–covariance structure or with limited dependent variables (LeSage 1997, 2000), for
spatial panel data models with random effects (Parent and LeSage 2012), and for undertaking
model comparisons (Hepple 1995b; LeSage and Parent 2007). The estimation of HSAR is
achieved via MCMC methods that draw samples sequentially from the conditional posterior
distributions for each model unknown parameter (Gelfand and Smith 1990; Smith and LeSage
2004). To implement HSAR, we need to specify the prior distributions for each parameter and
derive their conditional distributions.

Application of the HSAR model
In this section, we demonstrate the HSAR model using a data set that consists of all leased
residential land parcels from 2003 to 2009 in Beijing, China. There are 1,117 land parcels situated
in 111 districts (Jiedao). A detailed data description is provided by Harris, Dong, and Zhang
(2013). We regard land parcels as the lower level and districts as the regional level, thus giving
a two-level hierarchical data structure.
Spatial interactions in house or land markets have been well recognized in at least two ways.
The ﬁrst regards the actual workings of the real estate market, in which the sale prices of nearby
properties are often used to guide the price of a property for sale (e.g., Can 1992; Koschinsky,
Lozano-Gracia, and Piras 2012). The second is related to the interacting decision-making process
like house maintenance or repairing decisions among householders (Can and Megbolugbe 1997).
It is also expected that regional effects are dependent. For example, regions of close spatial
proximity might be similar in local tax rates, educational quality, public facility provision, and so
on, which exert similar effects on land prices.

Conclusions
In this article, we brought forward a research question: how to modify a standard spatial
econometric method, speciﬁcally the SAR model, to accommodate a geographically hierarchical
Geographical Analysis
14
data structure? To answer this question, we began by outlining a typology of four different model
speciﬁcations that are distinguished by the type of weights matrix used and whether the regional
effects are treated as ﬁxed or random. Previous research and implementations of these models
were discussed.
In this article, we have given extensive attention to the HSAR model because of its meth-
odological advantage that it allows for estimation of spatial spillover effects while also allowing
for the control and examination of dependent group effects. By simultaneously modeling the
spatial dependence and the group dependence, the HSAR model avoids the situation where these
two types of dependence are confounded. It provides more accurate estimates for both of them.
Our view is that the HSAR is especially well suited to the increasingly available hierarchical
data sets with detailed geographic information and for providing a greater synthesis between
multilevel modeling and spatial econometric perspectives (more so than the other model
speciﬁcations).

SAR model. In essence, HSAR just allows the model intercept term to have a spatial dependence
structure through the simultaneous autoregressive process of θ. The future extension could be to
allow for differentiating spatial processes for the regression coefﬁcients, which enables us to
